Research

Building clinically informed digital support — with careful evaluation, ethical governance, and patient safety at the centre.

Evidence

SimHealth is being developed as a clinical digital companion for people experiencing gambling-related urges. The research direction is guided by established therapeutic principles used in behavioural health, including skills-based strategies and exposure-informed approaches.

The goal of this programme is not to “prove an app works overnight”, but to explore whether structured digital support can: improve continuity of care, support safer decision-making during high-risk moments, and reduce disengagement between sessions.

Methodology

SimHealth is being designed for staged evaluation — beginning with small pilot settings and progressing only when safety, governance, and clinical oversight are in place. The programme is intended to support careful iteration, not uncontrolled release.

Data handling (privacy-first)

Where data collection is included in a pilot, it is intended to be strictly optional and used only with explicit patient consent. Any collection would be limited to what is necessary for evaluation and improvement, and designed to follow applicable rules in France and the EU (including GDPR principles).

  • Consent-led: participation and data sharing would be opt-in, with clear explanations and the ability to withdraw where applicable.
  • Minimised: collect the minimum necessary information for evaluation, avoiding unnecessary identifiers.
  • Protected: prioritise anonymisation where feasible; otherwise use pseudonymisation with strict access controls.
  • Governed: any research use would be subject to appropriate oversight, documentation, and ethics processes when required.

SimHealth is intended to be compatible with stringent privacy expectations in healthcare. Details of any specific pilot protocol, including legal basis, retention periods, and governance, would be defined with clinical partners and appropriate compliance guidance.

Why this matters

Gambling harm is often episodic: individuals can feel “stable” for long periods and then enter a short window of high risk. Research in this space benefits from tools that can support patients in those windows — while also enabling clinicians and researchers to better understand patterns (without compromising privacy).

Outcomes

Outcomes will only be discussed publicly when they are supported by appropriate evaluation. Early-stage pilots focus on feasibility, safety, and engagement — not medical claims.

  • Feasibility: can the tool be used safely and consistently within real clinical pathways?
  • Engagement: does it help patients stay connected between sessions or recovery milestones?
  • Safety signals: does the experience reduce harm risks rather than increase them?
  • Clinical usability: can clinicians integrate it without adding excessive burden?
  • Future research readiness: is the programme structured enough for larger studies if justified?

Note: SimHealth is in development and is not presented as a medical device or treatment. Any future research programme would be conducted with appropriate governance and professional guidance.
